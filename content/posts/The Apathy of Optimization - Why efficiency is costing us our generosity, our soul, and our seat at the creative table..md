Efficiency is a seductive religion.

It seeks to bring maximum value and production at little cost or time. It whispers that we can maximize every hour, automate every mundane task, and conquer an ever-growing laundry list of to-do items while "learning" three new skills on the side. 

I get that appeal.

But lately I have realized that the more we pursue this, we accidentally solve for a life without a soul.

It makes us [less creative](https://www.forbes.com/sites/johnkoetsier/2026/02/24/ai-is-making-us-less-creative-but-theres-a-fix/). And more importantly, we become less generous.

Generosity is the readiness to give *more* than what is strictly necessary or expected. In our rush to optimize, we have turned our lives into a series of transactions. We have machines do everything for us: respond to texts with a few words or sentences, summarize hours of reading into a podcast or paragraphs of reading, even write and create our posts online to feed the content machine.

The sheer *amount* of ideas and work that can be accomplished is incredible - I use these tools myself to spark a connection or bridge a gap.

But AI is incapable of generosity. It gives exactly what it is told to give. It never embellishes out of love; it never lingers on a detail out of devotion. It has no soul because it has no skin in the game.

When we offload these tasks, we aren't just saving time; we are withholding our presence.

There is a neural ignition that happens when you fire off a manual text to a friend, or struggle to put your own thoughts into words after reading a difficult chapter. It really is food for your brain. Connections are forged. Your brain sparks. **You are changed**.

When we give these seemingly mundane tasks to machines - read everything, not splitting the load - nothing happens inside of us. We don't learn, we don't feel, we don't innovate. We simply pass the data through a filter. 

The mundane is not a hurdle; it is the soil. It is the effort of cultivating the garden that is ultimately you.

I am all for saving time. This is actually why I chose to build applications to do such a thing, but it is also because life was about accomplishing one thing after the other. It was just work, but it wasn't fun - and it wasn't growing me at all.

---
When we lose our generosity with life, we lose our care.
- No more sour in our art.
- No more weight in our words.
- No more time to sit with ourselves and become who we want to be.
---

If all we do is look to save time, we forget what it means to be generous. We forget to give our time to others, to our work, to ourselves. And if we lose ourselves in the process of saving time, what exactly are we saving it for?
